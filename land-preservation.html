Active Stewardship

Protecting Land Is Just the Beginning

Acquiring land is an important first step, but the ongoing ecological health of the blufflands requires active, long-term stewardship. Native bluff prairies, oak woodlands, and sandstone cliff communities are under constant pressure from invasive plant species and decades of altered land use.

The coalition coordinates restoration initiatives to manage invasive species like buckthorn and honeysuckle, reestablish native plant communities, and maintain the biodiversity that makes this landscape so remarkable.

Hundreds of volunteers spend Saturday afternoons building trails, clearing invasive species, and restoring native habitat — a community effort that reflects deep local pride in this landscape.

What We Restore

Rare Habitats of the Upper Mississippi

The blufflands contain natural communities found nowhere else in the region — each requiring different approaches to restoration and stewardship.

🌼
Bluff Prairies
Rare native prairie communities on dry, south-facing slopes. Home to compass plant, butterfly milkweed, leadplant, and pasqueflower. Threatened by woody encroachment without active management.
🌳
Oak Woodlands & Savannas
Ancient bur and white oak ecosystems once covering blufftops. Restoration removes invasive shrubs and reintroduces prescribed fire to revive these fire-dependent communities.
🪨
Sandstone Cliff Communities
Unique plant communities on exposed sandstone outcrops. Sensitive to disturbance; harbor rare mosses, ferns, and cliff-dwelling species found throughout the driftless area.
🦅
Wildlife Corridors
Connected natural areas allow bald eagles, hawks, turkey vultures, white-tailed deer, and dozens of species to move freely through the landscape.
🚫
Invasive Species Control
Common buckthorn, honeysuckle, and other invasives suppress native species. Volunteers and staff work year-round to manage these persistent threats across the region.
🌊
Watershed Health
Healthy bluffland vegetation protects water quality in streams flowing to the Mississippi River, reduces erosion, and buffers communities from flooding.
The 2016 Blufflands Plan

Six Goals for a Thriving Region

01
Public Health
Improve outdoor access and active recreation for all residents and visitors.
02
Ecological Diversity
Restore and protect native plant and wildlife communities across the blufflands.
03
Connected Communities
Link cities, towns, and natural areas through a continuous trail network.
04
Economic Strength
Attract workforce, tourism, and investment through exceptional natural assets.
05
Quality of Life
Ensure these lands remain accessible and meaningful for future generations.
06
Regional Coordination
Unite public and private stakeholders under a shared, long-term conservation plan.
